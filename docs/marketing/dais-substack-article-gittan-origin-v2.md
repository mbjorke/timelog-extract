# I Used My Browser History as a Diary for Decades—Then AI Made My Weaknesses My Superpower

## Introduction: The Illusion of Control

We like to believe someone is in charge. We imagine that the CEOs earning billions each year have a steady hand on the wheel of AI.  
**In practice, no single person fully controls the system-level behavior.**

From the Silicon Valley elite to the person sending a first prompt today, we are all working with systems that can produce seemingly impossible outcomes at a pace we have never seen before.

For years, I treated my lack of "traditional mastery" as a liability. I'm not the best programmer in the room. I've been a treasurer and still felt lost reading parts of a balance sheet. My mental math peaked early and faded over time.

In the era of agentic AI, I've realized something:

**Being "naive" can be a superpower.**

Nerd footnote: Chrome's public beta shipped on September 2, 2008 (stable followed on December 11), and its open-source sibling was introduced as Chromium.

---

## The Naive Superpower

Why can people with basic programming experience suddenly build complex systems?

Because they are often less constrained by inherited rules about how things are "supposed" to be done. They focus on the problem, not the identity performance of being an expert in one stack.

When you don't worship the syntax, you can move on intent.  
When you don't defend old boundaries, you can orchestrate tools across them.

I spent years trying to suppress my aesthetic side, thinking "real engineering" should be purely dry and functional. Yesterday's chaos proved the opposite. In AI-heavy workflows, taste and feel are not decoration. They help structure ambiguity, reduce noise, and choose what matters.

People are now building and shipping **faster than ever**.  
Not because everything is easier, but because iteration loops are radically shorter.

---

## From Chaos to Proof: Why Gittan Exists

If no one fully controls AI behavior end-to-end, the real question is not control.  
The real question is trust.

That is where **Gittan** came from.

Not from technical elitism, but from survival and accountability:

- I needed to answer a simple but painful question: **Where did my time go?**
- I didn't want to reconstruct reality from memory.
- I didn't want to become a manual clock-puncher either.

So I built a local-first system that treats digital traces as external memory.

Gittan aggregates local traces from sources like browser history, AI/IDE activity, and worklogs into reviewable project-hour reports. If we cannot inspect every internal reasoning step of an AI workflow, we can still make the output chain auditable.

Think of it as a practical recorder for modern knowledge work: not perfect truth, but accountable evidence.

---

## The Burden of the "Easy" Task

The irony of this era is that the hardest tasks are often the "easy" ones: low-friction tasks with no real deadline, no risk, and no knot to untangle.

I am fueled by friction.  
Give me a multi-signal mess, an integration deadlock, a workflow that should not work but must work, and I come alive.  
Give me a simple linear task with no urgency, and I stall.

This is why observability matters beyond engineering hygiene. It creates structure for people who think in systems but struggle with empty, context-less tasks. It turns vague effort into inspectable progress.

---

## Conclusion: Trusting the Mess

We are moving from a world where expertise was mainly about deep specialization to one where leverage comes from orchestration.

My long browser history is not a nostalgia archive. It is evidence of how human work actually happens: nonlinear, interrupted, multi-context, and hard to summarize after the fact.

**The current Gittan release** is my way of saying:  
I do not need to be the best coder in the room if I can build the most trustworthy chain of evidence around the work.
